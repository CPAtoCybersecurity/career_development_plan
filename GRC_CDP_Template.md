#### Learn in Public: Start a Blog or YouTube Channel

The fastest way to accelerate your career is to learn in public. Document what you're doing as you take courses, read frameworks, and build projects.

**Why it works:** It creates proof of work for employers, attracts networking connections, forces deeper learning through teaching, and builds a searchable portfolio for recruiters.

You don't need to be an expert. Share what you're learning as you learn it. Your beginner perspective helps others who are one step behind you.

**Your Action Items:**

- [ ] Choose your platform (Substack, Medium, personal site, or YouTube)
- [ ] Pick one course or framework you're currently studying
- [ ] Commit to publishing one post or video per week
- [ ] Share your content in a community (e.g., Simply Cyber Discord) for feedback

**Inspiring Examples:**

|---|---|---|

#### Getting Started with AI & Developer Tooling

This section isn't about becoming a software engineer. It's about building the hands-on skills to work faster, automate the boring stuff, and contribute to real projects. GRC professionals who can use AI tools and collaborate on GitHub stand out immediately.

**Setup Checklist:**

- [ ] Install VS Code: https://code.visualstudio.com

You don't need to build AI systems, but you do need to understand how they work, what risks they introduce, and how to write policies around them. And using a coding agent to automate evidence collection or build a risk register template? That's the kind of thing that gets you noticed.

#### Find an Accountability Partner

The fastest way to stay consistent is to pair up with someone on the same path. Not a mentor (who's ahead of you) — a peer (who's right beside you).

**How to find one:**

1. Post in the Simply Cyber Discord: "Looking for an accountability partner working through the Career Development Plan"
2. Exchange career stories and current self-assessment scores
3. Agree to a weekly 15-minute check-in (voice or text — whatever works)

**Weekly Check-In Template:**

- What I did this week (be specific — name the task from your plan)
- What I'm doing next week (be specific — name the task)
- Where I'm stuck (be honest — that's the whole point)

**Ground Rules:**

- [ ] Check in every week, even if you did nothing (especially then)
- [ ] Be specific, not vague ("I commented on 3 LinkedIn posts" not "I networked")
- [ ] Celebrate wins — if your partner got something done, tell them it matters
- [ ] If someone goes silent for 2 weeks, reach out directly

> **Why this works better than solo study:** You'll skip a task when nobody's watching. You won't skip it if someone's asking you about it on Thursday.

#### Build Your Career Story (Tell It in 60 Seconds)

Every networking conversation, interview, and LinkedIn post is a version of the same story. Build yours using this structure:

**1. Where I started:** [Your previous career or background]

**2. Why I'm moving to GRC:** [The specific moment or realization that pulled you in]

**3. What I've done about it:** [Concrete actions — courses, projects, contributions, communities]

**4. Where I'm heading:** [Target role and what you'll bring to it]

> **Example:** "I spent five years as a financial auditor, where I kept seeing the same IT control failures show up in every engagement. That's what pulled me into cybersecurity GRC — I realized I could have more impact on the controls themselves rather than just testing them after the fact. I've since completed Security+, contributed to the NIST CSF Profile Assessment Database on GitHub, and I'm currently building a vendor risk assessment template as a portfolio project. I'm targeting GRC analyst roles where I can bring my audit methodology and business communication skills to security programs."

#### NIST CSF 2.0: Community Resources You Should Know

NIST maintains a curated list of community-submitted tools, profiles, and reference mappings for the Cybersecurity Framework 2.0. These are practitioner-built resources — not just the framework itself — and using them is one of the fastest ways to build real fluency with CSF.

**Start here:** [nist.gov/cyberframework/csf-20-resources](https://www.nist.gov/cyberframework/csf-20-resources-0)

**Featured: CSF Profile Assessment Database**
Created by **Steve McMichael** and **Gerald Auger** (Simply Cyber), the [CSF Profile Assessment Database](https://github.com/CPAtoCybersecurity/csf_profile) is an open-source tool listed on NIST.gov's official community resources page. It lets you build, score, and track CSF 2.0 profile assessments — the kind of work GRC analysts do on the job. Contributing to this project is a concrete way to build portfolio evidence, learn GitHub workflows, and demonstrate framework knowledge to employers. No coding experience required to contribute.
